HOW DOES A WINDMILL PRODUCE ELECTRICITY?

  The aerodynamic force of the blades,like a airplane, converts wind energy into electricity in a wind turbine. The air pressure on one side of the blade lowers when wind blows across it. Lift and drag are created by the difference in air pressure across the two sides of the blade. The lift force is greater than the drag force, causing the rotor to spin. The blades are attached to a drive shaft that rotates an electric generator, which provides power.

Find out more on Wind Energy

HOW DO WE HARNESS ENERGY FROM THE SUN?

In an hour and a half, the amount of sunshine that penetrates the earth's surface is enough to power the entire world's energy usage for a year. Photovoltaic (PV) panels or mirrors that concentrate solar radiation are used in solar technologies to convert sunlight into electrical energy. This energy can be converted into electricity or stored in a battery for long term usage.

Learn More about solar energy

HOW DO WE GET ENERGY FROM WATER?

Hydroelectric power stations are frequently built on or near a water source because water is the source of hydroelectric power. The amount of accessible energy in moving water is determined by the volume of the water flow and the change in elevation—or fall, as it is commonly known to as head—from one location to another. In general, the higher the head and the greater the water flow, the more electricity a hydropower plant can generate.

Cool Facts about Renewable Energy!

  • Wind

    Turbines are becoming taller in order to reach higher in the atmosphere, where quicker, more consistent winds can be found. The higher you fly, the faster the winds will be and the more energy you'll be able to generate!

  • Hydro

    Hydropower costs less than most energy sources. States that get the majority of their electricity from hydropower, like Idaho, Washington, and Oregon, have energy bills that are lower than the rest of the country.

  • Solar

    WORLD HEALTH BENEFITS FROM SOLAR POWER According to Google's Project Sunroof, which combines data from Google Maps and Google Earth with 3D modeling and machine learning, 79 percent of all homes in America have rooftops that are "technically viable" for solar, meaning they have enough roof area and receive enough sunlight.
